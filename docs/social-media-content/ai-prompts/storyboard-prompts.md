# Video Storyboard AI Prompts

## Overview
This document provides comprehensive AI prompts for creating detailed video storyboards for Asset Management Property's content. Storyboards help visualize your video before production, ensuring efficient shooting and professional results.

1. **Start with your script** (or concept if no script yet)

8. **Use during shooting** as reference guide

---

## What is a Storyboard?

A storyboard is a visual representation of your video, shot by shot. It includes:
- **Visual descriptions** of each scene/shot
- **Camera angles and movements**
- **Timing for each shot**
- **Dialogue/voiceover text**
- **On-screen text overlays**
- **Transitions between shots**
- **Music and sound effects cues**
- **Notes for production team**

## Technical Storyboard Elements

### Camera Angle Reference Guide

**Wide Shot (WS)**
- Purpose: Establish location, show full space
- Use: Property exteriors, room reveals
- Camera: Far from subject, showing environment
- Lens: Wide angle (16-35mm)

**Medium Shot (MS)**  
- Purpose: Presenter or focal point with context
- Use: Presenter segments, furniture groupings
- Camera: Waist-up (person) or partial space
- Lens: Standard (35-50mm)

**Close-Up (CU)**
- Purpose: Details, features, emotional connection
- Use: Finishes, appliances, fixtures, faces
- Camera: Close to subject, minimal background
- Lens: Standard to short telephoto (50-85mm)

**Extreme Close-Up (ECU)**
- Purpose: Emphasize quality, texture, detail
- Use: Materials, craftsmanship, luxury elements
- Camera: Very close, filling frame
- Lens: Macro or telephoto

**Over-the-Shoulder (OTS)**
- Purpose: Perspective, viewer involvement
- Use: Looking at view, entering spaces
- Camera: Behind presenter/viewer showing POV
- Lens: Various

**Point of View (POV)**
- Purpose: Viewer as participant, immersive
- Use: Walking through property, opening doors
- Camera: First-person perspective
- Lens: Wide angle for immersion

**Bird's Eye / Aerial**
- Purpose: Scale, location, unique perspective
- Use: Property overview, neighborhood context
- Camera: Drone or high angle
- Lens: Drone camera (varies)

### Camera Movement Reference

**Static / Locked-off**
- Camera doesn't move
- Use: Presenter talking, establishing shots
- Equipment: Tripod

**Pan** (Horizontal movement)
- Camera rotates left or right on axis
- Use: Revealing spaces, following movement
- Equipment: Tripod with fluid head

**Tilt** (Vertical movement)
- Camera rotates up or down on axis
- Use: Showing height, ceiling details
- Equipment: Tripod with fluid head

**Dolly** (Camera moves forward/backward)
- Camera travels toward or away from subject
- Use: Approaching property, entering rooms
- Equipment: Slider or gimbal walk

**Tracking** (Camera moves sideways)
- Camera travels parallel to subject
- Use: Following along features, maintaining perspective
- Equipment: Slider or gimbal walk

**Gimbal/Stabilizer Movement**
- Smooth handheld-style movement
- Use: Tours, following action, dynamic shots
- Equipment: Gimbal stabilizer (Ronin, DJI RS)

**Drone Movement**
- Aerial perspective, smooth flying
- Use: Exterior reveals, location context, cinematic shots
- Equipment: Drone (DJI Mini, Mavic, etc.)

**Zoom**
- Lens focal length changes (in or out)
- Use: Emphasize details, create tension (use sparingly)
- Equipment: Zoom lens or digital post-zoom

### Transition Reference

**Cut** (Instant transition)
- Most common, invisible
- Use: Between shots, changes in angle/location

**Fade to Black**
- Shot fades to black, then next shot fades in
- Use: Time passage, chapter changes

**Cross Dissolve**
- One shot fades into next
- Use: Gentle transitions, time passing, thematic connection

**Wipe**
- One shot pushes/replaces another
- Use: Dramatic transitions (use sparingly in real estate)

**Match Cut**
- Similar composition/element in consecutive shots
- Use: Smooth flow between spaces, creative transitions

**L-Cut / J-Cut**
- Audio leads or follows video
- Use: Natural flow, professional feel

**Swipe**
- Shot slides out as next slides in
- Use: Social media native feel, modern style

**Speed Ramp**
- Slow motion to normal to fast motion
- Use: Dynamic emphasis, modern style

## Storyboarding Best Practices

### Before You Start Storyboarding

1. **Have a script or detailed concept**
   - Know what you're visualizing
   - Understand the narrative arc
   - Identify key messages

2. **Understand your constraints**
   - Location access and timing
   - Equipment available
   - Budget limitations
   - Crew size and skills

3. **Know your platform requirements**
   - Aspect ratio (9:16 vs 16:9 vs 1:1)
   - Duration limits
   - Platform-native styles

4. **Research visual references**
   - Find examples of similar videos
   - Note what works well
   - Identify style preferences

### During Storyboarding

1. **Think in sequences, not just shots**
   - How shots flow together
   - Transitions between ideas
   - Pacing and rhythm

2. **Be specific but flexible**
   - Detailed enough to guide production
   - Flexible enough for on-set adjustments
   - Include alternatives for problem shots

3. **Consider practical production**
   - Group shots by location to save time
   - Note difficult/time-consuming shots
   - Identify shots that can be cut if needed

4. **Plan for post-production**
   - Where graphics will go
   - Text overlay space
   - Color grading needs
   - Music sync points

### Storyboard Review Checklist

Before production, review:
- [ ] Opening hook is strong (first 3 seconds)
- [ ] Clear visual flow and logic
- [ ] All key messages represented visually
- [ ] Sufficient variety in shots (not repetitive)
- [ ] Practical to shoot (realistic timing)
- [ ] Equipment needs identified
- [ ] Text overlays planned with space
- [ ] Music cues marked
- [ ] Transitions make sense
- [ ] CTA is clear and visible
- [ ] Duration matches platform requirements
- [ ] Brand elements included
- [ ] Shots grouped efficiently for production
- [ ] Backup shots identified
- [ ] Team understands the plan

## From Storyboard to Production

### Creating a Shot List from Storyboard

Once storyboard is approved, create a shot list organized by:

**Location** (shoot all shots in one place together)
```
LOCATION: Living Room

Shot 2: Entry POV walk (gimbal)
Shot 7: Sofa close-up (tripod)
Shot 15: Window view reveal (tripod)

Equipment: Gimbal, Tripod, Wide lens
Estimated time: 45 minutes
```

**Setup** (group by similar camera setup)
```
SETUP: Tripod wide shots

Shot 1: Exterior (tripod, wide angle)
Shot 15: Living room (tripod, wide angle)
Shot 22: Bedroom (tripod, wide angle)

Equipment: Tripod, 16-24mm lens
Move through property systematically
```

**Time of Day** (when natural light matters)
```
GOLDEN HOUR (5:30-6:00 PM):

Shot 1: Exterior drone
Shot 20: Balcony view
Shot 25: Pool area

Must shoot during this window!
Backup plan if cloudy: reschedule
```

### Pre-Production Meeting

Walk production team through storyboard:
- Show visual reference
- Explain intent of each sequence
- Discuss technical approach
- Identify challenges
- Assign responsibilities
- Confirm equipment needs
- Review timeline

### On-Set Use of Storyboard

- Print or have digital copy accessible
- Check off shots as completed
- Note any deviations or creative changes
- Capture backup shots/alternatives
- Review footage against storyboard before wrapping

### Post-Production Reference

- Editor uses storyboard as assembly guide
- Graphics team knows exactly where elements go
- Review cut against storyboard
- Identify any missing shots (reshoot or adjust)
- Maintain planned pacing and flow
